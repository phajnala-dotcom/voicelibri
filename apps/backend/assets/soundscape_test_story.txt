The Shadow of Thornwood Castle

Chapter 1: The Forest Path

The morning sun filtered through the dense canopy of ancient oaks, casting golden patterns on the moss-covered ground. Birds sang their chorus from every branch, a symphony of whistles and trills that filled the quiet forest. Elara adjusted the leather strap of her satchel and pressed forward along the narrow dirt path, her boots crunching on dry leaves with each careful step.

A twig snapped sharply underfoot, and she froze. In the silence that followed, she could hear the gentle rustling of wind through the leaves above. Somewhere far off, a woodpecker hammered rhythmically against a hollow trunk.

The path descended toward a rushing stream. The sound of water grew louder as she approached — a constant, churning flow over smooth rocks. She knelt at the bank and splashed cold water on her face, gasping at its icy bite. A frog croaked nearby and leaped into the water with a distinct plop.

Beyond the stream, the forest thinned. The cheerful birdsong faded behind her as she stepped into open meadowland. Wind swept across the tall grass in waves, carrying the distant bleating of sheep from a hillside farm. The sky above had darkened considerably. Thunder rumbled low on the horizon, a deep growl that promised rain.

She quickened her pace. The first heavy raindrops struck the ground around her, splattering against the stones of the old road. Within moments, the rain intensified into a downpour, hammering relentlessly against her cloak. Lightning cracked across the sky with a blinding flash, followed immediately by an explosive thunderclap that shook the earth beneath her feet.

Through the curtain of rain, she spotted the massive stone walls of Thornwood Castle rising from the hilltop. She ran the final distance and pounded her fist against the heavy oak door. The sound echoed through the entrance hall beyond. After a long moment, iron bolts scraped and clanked, and the great door groaned open on its ancient hinges.

Chapter 2: The Castle Within

The entrance hall was vast and cold. Elara's wet boots echoed on the flagstone floor as she stepped inside. The heavy door slammed shut behind her with a resonant boom that reverberated through the empty corridors. Somewhere deep in the castle, a clock struck seven — each chime ringing clear and solemn through the stone passages.

A fire crackled in the enormous hearth at the far end of the hall, casting flickering orange light across the tapestried walls. The wood popped and hissed, sending sparks spiraling upward into the blackened chimney. The warmth was a welcome relief from the storm that still raged outside, its muffled thunder barely penetrating the thick walls.

Footsteps approached from the corridor to her left — measured, deliberate steps on stone. An elderly man appeared, carrying a brass candelabra. The candle flames guttered as he walked, throwing dancing shadows across his weathered face.

"Follow me," he said, his voice low and gravelly.

He led her through a winding corridor lined with suits of armor. Their metal joints seemed to creak as she passed, though perhaps it was merely the wind finding cracks in the old masonry. A rat scurried across the floor ahead of them, its claws clicking rapidly on the stone before vanishing into a gap in the wall.

They climbed a narrow spiral staircase, each step groaning under their weight. At the top, the old man pushed open a wooden door that creaked on rusty hinges, revealing a small chamber. A window rattled violently against its frame as the storm howled outside. Rain lashed against the glass in fierce, staccato bursts.

He set the candelabra on the desk and struck a match, its sharp hiss cutting the silence before the flame caught and steadied. He lit two more candles, then turned to leave.

"Lock the door," he said quietly. "And do not open it until morning. No matter what you hear."

The door closed softly. She turned the iron key in the lock — a heavy, grinding click. Then she was alone, with nothing but the crackling fire, the howling wind, and the distant, rhythmic dripping of water somewhere in the walls.
